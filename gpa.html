Road to Ivy League

Grade Point Average

What it is, and how to improve yours

Unweighted GPA

An unweighted GPA is a measure of relative academic success that is based solely on grades, and not the difficulty of each class taken. The unweighted GPA is measured on a scale of 4 points. Thus, a score of 100 points in a class would earn you 4 points toward your average. Each point in a class average (0-100) corresponds to 0.1 points on a scale of 4 points. If you score a 90 in a class, you can count 3 points to your average. An 80 corresponds to a 2.0, and a 70 corresponds to a 1.0, but anything below 70 points will contribute nothing to your average. Your unweighted GPA is an average of each class' score on this 4 point scale.

Weighted GPA

A weighted GPA is a measure of relative academic success that takes into account the difficulty of each class taken. Your school will have typically two or three set levels that each class is classified as one of. Academic classes will be considered on a 4.0 scale, just as any class is for an unweighted GPA. Classes that are on an Honors or Pre-AP level will typically be considered on a scale of 5 points. For each point out of your 100 point class average that you deviate from a perfect 100, subtract 0.1 from 5 points. This will be your score for that class. If your school offers AP classes as well, these will likely be considered on a scale of 6 points. The score for this class is calculated just the same as the previous, except you will subtract 0.1 points from 6 rather than from 5 or 4. Your weighted GPA will be the average of each class' calculated score.


Importance of GPA


GPA is one of the primary considerations that colleges use to determine whether a student should be accepted or denied admission to their university. Most colleges will consider both your unweighted and weighted GPA's, so it is imperative that you do everything in your power to maintain both.



Cumulative GPA



Colleges will not see each year's weighted/unweighted GPA individually. Rather, they see the average of your GPA's from each year, both weighted and unweighted. Many students hear from their teachers and friends that junior year is most important, and without understanding what is meant by this, they often will justify putting forth less effort in the first half of high school on this basis. This is a tragic misunderstanding, however, because due to the nature of cumulative GPA, it is often too late to significantly change your GPA by the time you get to junior and senior year. If a student puts forth minimal effort in the first half of high school and earns a GPA representative of their lack of effort, they are usually stuck with a low GPA. This student can work to earn an exceptionally high GPA junior year as they should, but this will only count as one third of their cumulative GPA, with two thirds being calculated from their lower GPA earned in the first half of high school. It is much better for the student to hit the ground running on day 1 of freshman year as they will not need to make any adjustments or compensate later for a low GPA.
